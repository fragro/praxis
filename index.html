Strategic Vision

We are building the system that makes the old system irrelevant.

Not through insurrection. Not through electoral capture. Through the patient construction of functional democratic infrastructure that demonstrably outperforms existing institutions, until the question is no longer whether to change the system, but why the old one is still running at all.

What PRAXIS Is
PRAXIS is a practice, not a product. Participatory Revolutionary Autonomous eXperience and Infrastructure Stack. It is a methodology for composing existing open-source tools into democratic governance infrastructure, a civic engagement engine that makes organizing visible and progressive, and a playbook for running neighborhood assemblies that make real decisions about real resources. PRAXIS builds three things: the Civic Engine, the Assembly Playbook, and the integration glue that connects proven open-source tools into a coherent practice. Everything else is harvested from an ecosystem that already exists. If the network adopts a different name in the course of organizing, the practice is unchanged. PRAXIS is the methodology, not the brand.
Dual Power: The Strategic Framework
Building parallel institutions, not seizing existing ones
The concept of dual power (two governance structures coexisting in tension) originates with the 1917 Russian soviets operating alongside the Provisional Government. But PRAXIS draws on a radical transformation of the concept: not an unstable revolutionary crisis to be exploited by a vanguard, but the deliberate, patient construction of democratic counter-institutions that gradually make existing power structures irrelevant. The Zapatistas have been doing this for thirty years in Chiapas: building their own schools, clinics, and courts without asking the Mexican state's permission. The Kurdish democratic movement has been doing it in northern Syria: governing five million people through neighborhood communes without a centralized state. PRAXIS treats this as an infrastructure problem, and solves it as one.
The Win Condition
What victory actually means
Victory is not the overthrow of the federal government. Victory is not electoral. The win condition is: sufficient participation in parallel democratic self-governance that the legitimacy of existing institutions becomes functionally irrelevant. When a neighborhood assembly consistently makes superior resource allocation decisions than the city council. When a cooperative network provides better employment conditions than the corporate sector. When mutual aid reaches people faster than FEMA. When the mesh network remains operational when the internet goes down. When communities begin asking not whether to change the system, but why they are still maintaining the old one at all. That is victory, not in the dramatic sense, but in the structural sense. The old system becomes a shell that the new one has already replaced from within.

Strategic Advantages
Hardest test environment first
PRAXIS is designed to produce results in the most politically hostile environments. If the model works where conditions are least favorable, it carries immediate credibility everywhere else. The harder the test environment, the more transferable the proof of concept.
Infrastructure-first, not protest-first
Minneapolis 2020 demonstrated what happens when protest energy erupts without pre-existing democratic infrastructure to absorb it: neighborhood assemblies formed, mutual aid networks served 18,000 people, a city council supermajority pledged to defund police. Within eighteen months the energy dissipated, volunteers burned out, and the police budget grew. PRAXIS does not begin with demands. It begins with functional alternatives. The practice exists before the crisis. The infrastructure catches the energy that would otherwise dissipate.
The crisis as accelerant
Economic destabilization, threats to electoral legitimacy, and accelerating institutional failure do not require manufactured urgency. The system is demonstrating its own failure in real time. PRAXIS needs only to be ready, findable, and deployable when people seek alternatives.
Federation not franchise
Every new city node is autonomous. The practice scales through coordination without requiring compliance. This is how open-source ecosystems scale, not how political organizations scale. PRAXIS is the methodology. Local groups are the implementations.
PRAXIS Manifesto Section 01 — Vision
Metaphysical Infrastructure

The values are the architecture.

Before the tools, before the assemblies, before the federation, there is a question that every political project must answer and that most refuse to ask: what kind of people are we trying to become? PRAXIS is not values-neutral infrastructure. The ethics are load-bearing. They are not decorative language applied after the system is designed. They are the design constraints that determine what the system can and cannot be.

Relational Existence
We exist in relationship, not as isolated individuals
The dominant Western political tradition begins with the autonomous individual and asks how individuals can be organized into a society. Indigenous political traditions begin with relationship and ask how the web of mutual obligation can be sustained across generations. PRAXIS begins with the second question. The Haudenosaunee (the Iroquois Confederacy, which has governed six nations across what is now the northeastern United States for centuries) structures decision-making around the principle that every action must be evaluated against its consequences for the next seven generations. Not the next quarter. Not the next election cycle. Seven generations. The Zapatistas' governance emerges from Tzeltal and Tojolabal Maya traditions in which authority resides in the collective, not in individuals, and in which work itself is understood as something the community decides together: what needs doing, who does it, and how. PRAXIS does not treat community as an aggregation of self-interested actors. It treats community as the primary unit of existence, and individuals as people who become fully themselves through participation in that community.
Reciprocity, Not Transaction
The gift economy as the foundation of democratic life
Market exchange is transactional: I give you something, you give me something of equivalent value, and our obligation to each other ends. Reciprocity is relational: I give because I am part of a web of mutual obligation that sustains everyone in it, including me. Cultures across the world have built entire economies on this principle. In the Pacific Northwest, communities held ceremonial feasts where leaders gave away their wealth to strengthen social bonds, understanding that generosity created obligation and obligation created community. In the Andes, neighbors exchange labor on each other's land, knowing that the help they give today will come back when they need it. In Aotearoa, the Maori practice of reciprocal obligation structures relationships across generations, balancing what is given and what is owed so that no one accumulates at the expense of others. These are not primitive precursors to market economics. They are sophisticated social technologies for maintaining community cohesion across generations. Time banking, mutual aid, cooperative labor, and commons-based resource sharing are modern implementations of the same principle. PRAXIS's approach to economics is designed around reciprocity, not transaction. Civic Points are a recognition system for contribution, not a currency for exchange. The Civic Engine tracks what people give, not what they are owed.
Mutual Aid as Structural Principle
Solidarity, not charity
The evolutionary record demonstrates that cooperation is as fundamental to survival as competition, and that the species which thrive are the ones that develop the most effective systems of mutual support. This is not a metaphor. It is biology applied to political organization. Charity preserves hierarchy: the giver remains above, the receiver remains below, and the structural conditions that produced the need remain unchanged. Mutual aid dissolves hierarchy: we give to each other because our fates are bound together, and the structures we build are designed to make the need for charity obsolete. The COVID-era mutual aid networks that fed 18,000 people in Minneapolis were not charities. They were communities recognizing that their survival depended on each other. PRAXIS exists to make that recognition permanent and structural rather than temporary and crisis-driven.
The Steady State
An economy that serves life instead of consuming it
The core pathology of capitalism is its structural "grow or die" imperative: a company that does not expand is consumed by one that does, and the result is an economy that must grow to survive. An economy that must expand will consume every commons, exhaust every resource, and commodify every relationship until there is nothing left. This is not a moral failing of individuals. It is a structural requirement of the system. The alternative is not austerity. It is sufficiency. A steady-state economy does not pursue endless growth. It pursues balance: meeting everyone's needs without exceeding the planet's capacity to regenerate. Think of it as a doughnut. Below a certain threshold, people lack the basics (food, housing, healthcare, education, political voice). Above a certain ceiling, economic activity destroys the ecological systems that sustain life (climate, biodiversity, freshwater, soil). The goal is to operate in the space between the floor and the ceiling: enough for everyone, not too much for the planet. Indigenous economies operated in this space for millennia, taking only what could be replenished. In northern Syria, cooperatives sell bread below production cost because feeding people matters more than profit. In Chiapas, organic agriculture and solidarity trade networks sustain communities without growth imperatives. PRAXIS is designed to support communities that measure prosperity by whether everyone has enough, not by whether the total is growing.
Kindness as Load-Bearing Structure
Systems designed so that care is the default, not the exception
Kindness is not sentimentality. It is a design constraint. A system in which helping your neighbor requires extraordinary effort is a system designed to isolate people. A system in which helping your neighbor is the path of least resistance is a system designed for solidarity. The Beatitudes are a political program, not a Sunday school lesson: blessed are the poor, the meek, the peacemakers, the merciful, those who hunger for justice. In the 1930s, a group of Catholic radicals took this literally: they opened houses of hospitality in slums, started communal farms, and ran direct action campaigns for decades on the principle that you feed the person in front of you and organize so that no one goes hungry. Across Latin America, priests and theologians built an entire movement on the recognition that the Gospel, read honestly, is a manual for building community from the bottom up: one where the last are first, where power flows upward from the people, and where the measure of a society is how it treats those with the least. PRAXIS encodes this by making mutual aid, facilitation, and community defense the highest-value actions in the Civic Engine. The system rewards care.
Restorative Justice
Justice that heals instead of justice that cages
The United States incarcerates more people than any nation in human history. The carceral system does not reduce harm. It concentrates harm in poor communities and communities of color, destroys families, and produces people who are less capable of healthy social participation upon release than they were before. It is a system designed to punish, not to restore. Restorative justice begins with a different question. Instead of asking "what law was broken and how should the offender be punished," it asks "who was harmed, what do they need, and whose obligation is it to meet that need?"
The evidence is not theoretical
The Zapatistas operate a three-tiered justice system that uses no imprisonment. First offenses receive mediation and counsel. Second offenses require community service. Repeated offenses involve the full assembly. The focus is on transforming the conditions that produced the harm, healing the relationship, and reintegrating the person into community life. In Rojava, the commune peace committees resolved over 20,000 disputes in a single year, compared to 4,500 handled by formal courts. The Women's Houses (Mala Jin) provide a parallel justice track for gender-based violence, operated by women for women. These systems produce lower recidivism, higher community satisfaction, and actual accountability, because accountability to your neighbors is more powerful than accountability to a distant court.
PRAXIS and restorative practice
PRAXIS is not a justice system. But the Assembly Playbook must include processes for handling conflict, harm, and accountability within assemblies and between participants. Those processes will be restorative, not punitive. Harm circles, community mediation, graduated accountability, and reintegration pathways are governance features, not optional add-ons. The Facilitator role includes training in restorative practice. The assembly documents harm and resolution transparently (with appropriate privacy protections) so that the community can see that accountability is real and that people who cause harm are supported in changing, not discarded. A movement that reproduces the carceral logic of the system it opposes has already failed on its own terms.
The Synthesis
These values are not aspirations. They are engineering requirements. Every technical decision, every governance process, every facilitation methodology, and every quest in the Civic Engine must be evaluated against them. Does this feature deepen reciprocity or enable extraction? Does this process restore relationships or punish individuals? Does this economic structure serve sufficiency or demand growth? Does this design make kindness easier or harder? If the answer to any of these questions points away from the values, the design is wrong, regardless of its technical elegance. The Zapatistas say todo para todos, nada para nosotros: everything for everyone, nothing for ourselves. That is not a slogan. It is a specification.
PRAXIS Manifesto Section 02 — Values
Theoretical Foundations

The intellectual lineage of the practice.

PRAXIS is not a novel political theory. It is the practical implementation of ideas that have been tested (in assemblies, in autonomous territories, in revolutionary experiments) across centuries and continents. The methodology directly encodes the structural lessons of these movements: what worked, what failed, and why.

Murray Bookchin: Communalism and Libertarian Municipalism
The municipality as the unit of revolution
Murray Bookchin (1921-2006) argued that the municipality (the city, town, or neighborhood) is the scale at which face-to-face democracy remains possible, and therefore the only viable arena for radical politics. His program, libertarian municipalism, proposes replacing the nation-state with a confederation of self-governing municipalities organized through popular assemblies. Citizens gather at the neighborhood level to deliberate and make binding decisions. Assemblies formulate policy; confederal councils and mandated, recallable delegates merely administer it. This distinction between policy and administration is fundamental: delegates carry strict instructions from their assemblies, not independent judgment.
Social ecology: hierarchy as the root crisis
Bookchin's philosophical foundation, social ecology, holds that nearly all ecological problems arise from deep-seated social problems. The domination of nature by humans stems from the domination of human by human. Capitalism's structural "grow or die" imperative makes ecological destruction systemic, not merely attitudinal. The solution is not spiritual transformation or technological fixes within capitalist markets, but decentralized, directly democratic, confederal institutions, the political form that embodies complementarity rather than domination.
Communalism: beyond both Marxism and anarchism
In his later work, Bookchin broke with anarchism and articulated communalism as a third position. Unlike anarchism, communalism accepts democratic institutions, majority rule, and structured organization as necessary for liberation, rejecting consensus process as impractical at scale and vulnerable to what happens when groups refuse to create formal structure: informal hierarchies emerge anyway, controlled by whoever has the most social capital, charisma, or free time. Unlike Marxism, it remains fundamentally anti-state and decentralist, rejecting the seizure of state power. Unlike both, it centers the municipality (not the factory, not the party) as the locus of political life. Power necessarily exists in every society; the question is whether it is exercised by an oligarchy or by the collective citizenry through direct democratic participation.
PRAXIS encodes Bookchin's architecture directly. The Assembly Playbook implements the popular assembly through governance tools like Decidim and Consul. The federation practice implements confederalism: power flowing upward from assemblies, not downward from central authority. The cooperative tools municipalize the economy under democratic control. The design principle of local autonomy with shared methodology is federation, not franchise, exactly as Bookchin prescribed.
Rojava: Democratic Confederalism in Practice
Bookchin's theory, implemented at the scale of millions
In the autonomous regions of North and East Syria (Rojava), the Kurdish freedom movement underwent a dramatic transformation. Its imprisoned leader, reading Bookchin from a cell on a Turkish island, abandoned the goal of founding an independent Kurdish state and adopted a new vision: democratic self-governance without a state at all. He called himself Bookchin's "student." The movement reorganized entirely around this idea. The result: a functioning multi-ethnic, multi-religious democratic governance system serving approximately five million people, built from the ground up during an active civil war while simultaneously defeating ISIS.
The commune as the base unit of governance
Rojava's system operates through five ascending tiers: the commune (100-350 households), the people's house (coordinating multiple communes), the city council, the canton assembly, and the inter-canton coordination assembly. The operating principle is subsidiarity: decisions are made at the lowest level possible and only escalate upward when necessary. Higher bodies cannot override communes. Over 1,500 communes operate across the territory, each running six committees: women's, youth, social, peace and reconciliation, self-defense, and economic. In 2014 alone, communes resolved over 20,000 disputes, compared to 4,500 handled by formal courts.
Women's liberation as structural architecture, not policy addition
Rojava added what Bookchin did not center: women's liberation as a co-equal pillar alongside democracy and ecology. Every leadership position at every level requires two co-chairs, one of whom must be a woman. But representation is only half the architecture. Women also operate a completely parallel governance structure: their own communes, their own councils, and their own justice houses that handle gender-based violence and family disputes, staffed and run entirely by women. This means women have equal voice in the general system and autonomous authority in a separate system. It is not quota politics. It is a dual-track governance architecture that makes patriarchal consolidation structurally impossible.
The cooperative economy
Rojava's economics operate on three property categories: public (natural resources, infrastructure), direct-use (individuals use but cannot sell), and collective (worker-managed). Cooperatives follow strict democratic principles: one member, one vote. Profits are split: 30% reinvested, 50% distributed to workers, 20% allocated to community needs. Some cooperatives deliberately operate at a loss for social good. Bread cooperatives produce at 100 lira and sell at 60. Approximately one-third of industry is worker-managed, and three-quarters of formerly private property is now under collective or communal use.
PRAXIS learns from Rojava: The commune structure maps directly to the neighborhood assembly model in the Assembly Playbook. The co-chair and gender parity requirements inform PRAXIS's facilitation methodology. The cooperative economy model informs which economic tools PRAXIS recommends. And the lesson of Rojava's vulnerability (military dependence on external powers, economic embargo, lack of international federation) is precisely what PRAXIS's approach to resilient, distributed tool composition is designed to solve.
The Zapatistas: Thirty Years of Autonomous Governance
Mandar obedeciendo: lead by obeying
Since 1994, the Zapatista Army of National Liberation (EZLN) has maintained autonomous governance across territories in Chiapas, Mexico, building their own schools, clinics, courts, and cooperatives entirely outside the Mexican state. Their foundational governance principle, mandar obedeciendo, means that leaders are delegates who implement community assembly decisions, not representatives who exercise independent judgment. All authority positions are unpaid and rotating. Communities retain the right to revoke mandates at any time. Seven governing maxims guide the practice: propose, don't impose; represent, don't supplant; convince, don't defeat; construct, don't destroy; everything for everyone, nothing for ourselves.
Autonomy without state capture
The Zapatistas' most radical contribution to revolutionary thought: they do not seek to seize state power. This breaks with the entire tradition of twentieth-century revolutionary movements. Their position is that the state is not the solution but part of the problem. Build autonomous structures that make the state irrelevant in your territory. Provide your own education, health, justice, and governance, and the state becomes unnecessary. The argument, made powerfully by theorists studying the Zapatista experiment, is that any movement that seizes state power is bound to replicate the systems of power it sought to eliminate, because the state is an architecture of domination regardless of who occupies it. The Zapatistas do not pursue power over others. They pursue the power to do: the collective capacity to build, to govern, to provide for each other without permission from above.
Restorative justice and autonomous institutions
The Zapatista justice system is restorative, not punitive: free of charge, conducted in indigenous languages, and widely recognized as less corrupt than state courts. Three levels of resolution escalate from community mediation through elected honor commissions to regional councils. Imprisonment is not used as punishment. The autonomous education system runs hundreds of schools with community-selected teachers, curriculum decided collectively, taught in indigenous languages. Healthcare is universal and free, combining indigenous medicine with modern practice. These are not aspirational programs. They have been operating for over three decades.
Constant self-reinvention
The Zapatistas have restructured their governance at least three times, most recently in 2023, dissolving the Juntas de Buen Gobierno and replacing them with hyperlocal GAL (Local Autonomous Governments) that can function independently if cut off by violence or disaster. This willingness to restructure based on honest self-assessment, rather than defending existing organizational forms, is a survival trait. The EZLN spent a full decade (1983-1993) organizing among 500 communities before their public emergence. Deep community roots, not vanguard ideology, explain their longevity.
PRAXIS encodes Zapatista lessons: The emphasis on tools that work offline and degrade gracefully mirrors the Zapatista principle of autonomous local operation even when cut off from the network. The Civic Engine's peer verification system implements a digital form of mandar obedeciendo: the community validates the work, not a central authority. The commitment to restructuring when structures fail, rather than defending institutional forms, is a core design principle. The willingness to swap tools, adopt new ones, and abandon what no longer serves the practice mirrors the Zapatistas' three governance restructurings in thirty years.
The Assembly Tradition: A Lineage, Not an Invention
→
Athenian Ekklesia
The original direct democratic assembly: all male citizens participating in collective governance of the polis. Bookchin drew on the Athenian concept of politics as direct self-management, contrasting it with modern "statecraft" in which citizens are reduced to passive constituents.
→
New England Town Meetings
Among the only surviving modern institutions where citizens regularly participate in face-to-face assemblies that deliberate binding decisions. Every resident shows up, debates the budget, votes on local law. Observers of American democracy from its earliest years recognized that these meetings trained citizens for broader participation, that self-governance is a skill developed through practice, not a right passively held. There is a continuous thread running from these township meetings through the Paris Commune, through the German workers' councils of 1918, through every moment in modern history when people have gathered in their neighborhoods and governed themselves directly.
→
Paris Commune (1871)
Elected council members were considered mandated delegates, recallable at any time, an unprecedented step. At least 28 revolutionary clubs operated across Paris arrondissements, open every evening, functioning as the living link between the mass movement and the Communal Council. The Commune demonstrated both the power and the fragility of municipal self-governance.
→
Spanish Revolution (1936)
During the Spanish Civil War, workers in Barcelona and Valencia defeated the military coup and, in the vacuum where state power had collapsed, organized themselves. Neighborhood committees ran the city. Factories were managed by the workers in them. In Catalonia, 75% of the economy came under worker control. Neighborhood cooperatives served as gathering places, meeting halls, and communal kitchens simultaneously, each one performing multiple civic functions from a single space. The integrated infrastructure model that PRAXIS's seven layers formalize digitally.
→
Porto Alegre Participatory Budgeting (1989)
The model that proved citizens can allocate real public money through democratic assemblies at city scale. Results in Porto Alegre: sewer and water connections rose from 75% to 98%, health and education budgets increased from 13% to 40%, the number of schools quadrupled, and participation grew from under 1,000 to 40,000 per year. Over 11,500 municipal PB processes now operate globally. PRAXIS's governance layer is built on Decidim, the open-source platform born from Barcelona's implementation of this model.
→
Occupy Wall Street (2011): What Worked and What Failed
Occupy's general assemblies demonstrated that consensus process at mass scale produces slow decision-making, unclear coordination, and vulnerability to disruption, precisely the failure Bookchin predicted when he broke with anarchism over the consensus question. PRAXIS adopts Bookchin's position: majority-rule democracy in assemblies, with minority rights to continued advocacy, not consensus paralysis. Occupy also proved that protest energy without pre-existing institutional infrastructure dissipates. PRAXIS is the infrastructure that Occupy needed and did not have.
→
Chilean Cabildos Abiertos (2019)
During Chile's 2019 uprising (triggered by a transit fare increase that became a mass rejection of the entire economic model), over 300 self-convened neighborhood assemblies erupted across the country within weeks. Chileans call them cabildos abiertos, open town halls. These spontaneous assemblies demonstrated the latent demand for direct democratic participation, and the structural limitation: without persistent infrastructure, the assemblies were short-lived and their energy was channeled into a constitutional process that ultimately failed. PRAXIS provides the persistent infrastructure that makes spontaneous assemblies into durable institutions.
→
Minneapolis (2020): The Lesson of Dissipation
After George Floyd's murder, Minneapolis saw neighborhood assemblies, mutual aid networks serving 18,000 people, and a city council supermajority pledging to dismantle the police department. Within eighteen months: volunteer exhaustion collapsed the mutual aid networks, the ballot measure to replace police failed 56-44, council members retreated from their commitments, and the police budget grew from $181M to $210M. Minneapolis is the definitive case study for why protest energy without persistent democratic infrastructure produces temporary spectacle, not structural transformation. PRAXIS exists so the next Minneapolis has somewhere to go.
The Synthesis
What PRAXIS takes from each tradition
From Bookchin: the neighborhood assembly as the fundamental unit of governance; confederalism as the scaling mechanism; majority-rule democracy over consensus paralysis; the municipality as the arena of transformation. From Rojava: the commune model proven at the scale of millions; gender parity as structural architecture; cooperative economics under democratic control; the lesson that international federation is a survival requirement, not an aspiration. From the Zapatistas: thirty years of proof that autonomous governance outside the state is sustainable; mandar obedeciendo as a design principle for accountability; restorative justice; the willingness to restructure when structures fail; the principle that revolution is a question, not an answer. From Porto Alegre: empirical evidence that participatory budgeting produces superior outcomes at city scale. From Occupy and Minneapolis: the negative proof that protest without infrastructure, consensus without structure, and energy without institutions produces nothing durable. PRAXIS is the technological infrastructure that these traditions required and did not have.

Core Design Principles
No single point of failure: ecosystem resilience
Because each function is filled by independent tools from a diverse ecosystem, no single tool failing can bring down the practice. If a governance platform stops being maintained, the community migrates to another. If a communication channel is compromised, the community switches. If a hosting provider shuts down, the data moves. This is the same principle that makes biological ecosystems robust: diversity of species, redundancy of function, no monoculture. The FBI's domestic counterintelligence program systematically destroyed the Black Panther Party by targeting centralized leadership. The Zapatistas survived because they were rooted in 500 communities, not a central committee. PRAXIS survives because it is a practice distributed across an ecosystem of tools, not a product hosted on a single server.
Local autonomy, shared methodology
Each community chooses its own tools, structures its own assemblies, and organizes its own economy. What is shared is the Assembly Playbook, the Civic Engine, and the integration guides that help communities compose tools into a coherent practice. The network generates value through coordination without requiring compliance. Bookchin was explicit: confederalism means the communities themselves are sovereign, not subordinate to a central body. Power flows upward, not downward. Three structural safeguards prevent recentralization: mandated recallable delegates, policy-making that stays local, and structural interdependence through mutual need rather than hierarchical command.
Accessible to non-technical communities within one month
A group of ten committed people with no technical background must be able to set up their tools and run their first assembly within one month, using the Assembly Playbook and integration guides. PRAXIS provides recommended tool configurations, step-by-step setup guides, and a support network of communities that have already done it. The Zapatistas spent a decade building community roots before their public emergence. PRAXIS must compress that timeline through accessible methodology, because crises do not wait for ten-year organizing campaigns.
PRAXIS Manifesto Section 04 — Architecture
Political Authority

Assemblies without power are book clubs.

The most beautifully designed governance platform in the world is worthless if the decisions it produces have no binding force. People will attend one assembly out of curiosity. They will attend a second if the first one was good. They will not attend a fifth if nothing they decided in the first four actually changed anything. The question that determines whether PRAXIS succeeds or dies is not technical. It is political: how do assemblies get teeth?

There is no single path to political authority. Every path has been tried, and every path has failed in specific, instructive ways. The answer is not to pick the right path. The answer is to run all of them simultaneously, because each path's weakness is covered by another path's strength. And the fight itself, even when it loses, is the organizing. A campaign that fails at the ballot box but turns out 10,000 people has built a list, trained a cohort, and demonstrated a constituency that did not visibly exist before. Even in defeat, the assembly that ran the campaign is stronger than it was before the campaign started. The movement that only fights battles it can win never fights at all.
The Five Paths to Political Authority
1
Petitions and Ballot Initiatives
The lowest-friction path to binding authority. An assembly decides that the city should allocate 10% of its police budget to community mental health response. The assembly drafts a ballot initiative. Participants collect 10,000 signatures. The measure goes on the ballot. If it wins, it is law. If it loses, the assembly now has a list of 10,000 people who cared enough to sign, a cohort of canvassers who learned how to talk to strangers about policy, and documented proof that a constituency exists. The next initiative starts from a stronger position than the first one did.
→
The pitfall
Petitions get ignored. City councils table them. Legal challenges drain resources. Signature thresholds are designed to be high enough to discourage exactly this kind of direct democracy. And even when an initiative passes, implementation depends on the same bureaucracy the initiative was designed to circumvent. Porto Alegre proved that participatory budgeting can transform a city. Porto Alegre also proved that when the political party that supported PB lost power, the new government suspended the program entirely. Legal victories without ongoing political power are reversible. Petitions are a tool, not a strategy.
2
Electoral Municipalism
Run candidates for city council. Not to participate in the system as it exists, but to use the system's own mechanisms to transfer authority out of it. The specific demand: charter changes that give neighborhood assemblies binding authority over portions of the municipal budget. This is the strategy that Bookchin prescribed. A city council member elected on a PRAXIS platform does not go to council meetings to represent constituents. They go to council meetings to demand that decision-making authority be moved from the council chamber to the neighborhood assembly. They use the levers of municipal government to hollow out municipal government. Electoral participation follows established community momentum. It never precedes it.
→
The pitfall
Elections co-opt. A candidate who runs on a platform of transferring power to assemblies arrives at city hall and discovers that the system is designed to absorb radical energy and redirect it into procedural channels. Committee assignments, coalition politics, budget negotiations, and the daily grind of governance pull even the most committed council member toward operating within the system rather than dismantling it. Minneapolis council members stood on a stage and pledged to defund the police. One of them later said he "got got" at the rally, admitting he had been swept up in the moment and committed to something he could not deliver. Electoral politics rewards people who are good at getting elected, and the skills that win elections are not the same skills that dismantle institutions. Every candidate must go in knowing they will be pressured to become a normal politician, and the assembly that sent them must maintain enough independent power to hold them accountable when that pressure hits.
3
Autonomous Governance
Operate outside the state. Do not ask permission. Provide the services the state does not, and provide them better. When the assembly coordinates mutual aid that reaches people faster than FEMA, when the cooperative network provides better jobs than the corporate sector, when the mesh network stays up when the internet goes down, the assembly has authority because it has earned it through competence. This is the Zapatista path: build your own schools, your own clinics, your own courts, and the state becomes irrelevant in your territory not because you overthrew it but because you outperformed it. This is also the path that Rojava walked: communes that resolved 20,000 disputes while the courts handled 4,500. The people's system outperformed the professional one, and authority followed competence.
→
The pitfall
Autonomous zones get crushed. The state does not passively accept the construction of parallel authority within its borders. The Zapatistas have survived for thirty years because they operate in remote mountain territory with international solidarity networks and because the Mexican government calculated that military reconquest would cost more than tolerance. Rojava survived as long as it had a superpower military alliance, and when that alliance weakened, Turkey invaded. In American cities, autonomous spaces face legal pressure, police intervention, and the steady attrition of operating without access to the tax base and institutional resources the state controls. Autonomy without the capacity to defend itself is temporary. The autonomous path must always be combined with the other paths, because political authority that exists entirely outside the legal framework is authority that the legal framework can destroy at will.
4
Direct Action and Confrontation
When the system will not negotiate, you confront it. Strikes. Occupations. Blockades. Marches that shut down the street because the street belongs to the people who walk on it. Direct action is how every right you currently possess was won. The eight-hour day was not legislated into existence by sympathetic politicians. It was extracted through strikes that cost workers their wages, their safety, and sometimes their lives. The Civil Rights Act was not the product of polite discourse. It was the product of sit-ins, boycotts, freedom rides, and the willingness to endure violence rather than accept injustice. Direct action communicates a political reality that petitions and elections cannot: that a constituency exists, that it is organized, and that it will not wait for permission to assert its interests.
→
The pitfall
Direct action burns people out. It exposes participants to legal risk, physical danger, economic retaliation, and the emotional weight of sustained confrontation with systems that have more resources than you do. The 2020 uprisings were the largest protest movement in American history, and within two years the energy was gone because there was no institutional infrastructure to absorb it. People showed up, gave everything they had, and went home exhausted to find that nothing structural had changed. Direct action without an institutional home to return to produces temporary spectacle and permanent burnout. The First Responder class in the Civic Engine exists precisely to give direct action a structured context: people who put their bodies on the line are recognized, supported, and connected to a community that sustains them beyond the action itself.
5
Moral Authority and Narrative Power
There is a form of power that is not legal, not electoral, and not physical: the power of being visibly, undeniably right. When an assembly publishes its budget analysis and the analysis is better than the city council's, that is moral authority. When a community mutual aid network responds to a disaster three days before FEMA arrives, that is moral authority. When the participatory budgeting process produces outcomes that measurably outperform the outcomes produced by professional administrators, that is moral authority. Moral authority does not compel the state to act. But it makes the state's refusal to act visible, and visibility is the precondition for every other path. The Culture Jammer and Artist classes exist to make this visibility a systematic output of the practice, not an accident that happens when a journalist shows up.
→
The pitfall
Being right is not enough. You can be right for decades and change nothing. Moral authority without political organization is a newspaper editorial. It generates sympathy without generating power. The history of progressive movements is littered with causes that were morally unassailable and politically ineffective because they never built the organizational capacity to translate moral clarity into binding political outcomes. Narrative power must be connected to every other path: the story supports the petition, the petition funds the campaign, the campaign builds the base, the base sustains the direct action, and the direct action produces the narrative. None of them work alone.
The Dialectic
All five paths run simultaneously. The fight is the organizing.
The synthesis is not to choose a path. It is to run all five at once and let them reinforce each other. The assembly runs a ballot initiative (Path 1) and the canvassing builds the base for an electoral campaign (Path 2). The electoral campaign demands charter changes that formalize the assembly's autonomous governance (Path 3). When the charter changes are blocked, direct action applies pressure (Path 4). The entire process is documented and published, building the moral authority (Path 5) that makes the next ballot initiative start from a stronger position. Each path feeds the others. Each failure produces organizational capacity that the next attempt builds on.
Even in defeat, good times can be found
A movement that only fights battles it knows it can win is a movement that never fights. The ballot initiative that loses 60-40 trained fifty canvassers, identified ten thousand supporters, and forced the city council to publicly argue against community mental health funding in an election year. The occupation that lasted three days before police cleared it put forty strangers in a park together, and twenty of them are still organizing two years later. The council candidate who lost by eight points built a volunteer operation that the next candidate inherits. The fight is not a cost to be minimized. The fight is the organizing. It is where people discover what they are capable of, where relationships are forged under pressure, and where the movement becomes real to the people in it. The Zapatistas say preguntando caminamos: asking, we walk. They do not say they walk toward guaranteed victory. They say they walk while asking questions, and the walking itself is the point. PRAXIS is designed so that every campaign, every action, every fight, win or lose, produces data, produces relationships, produces Civic Points, and produces a community that is stronger afterward than it was before. The system rewards the fight, not just the victory.
The assembly is the constant. The paths are the variables.
Through every path, in every campaign, the neighborhood assembly remains. Politicians come and go. Ballot initiatives pass and fail. Occupations rise and are cleared. Press cycles spike and fade. But the assembly meets next Tuesday, regardless. It met last Tuesday. It will meet the Tuesday after that. The assembly is not a tactic deployed for a campaign. It is the permanent democratic institution from which all campaigns emerge and to which all campaigns return. This is what Bookchin meant by dual power as ongoing practice rather than revolutionary crisis. This is what the Zapatistas mean by building the world you want without waiting for permission. The assembly is the lifeboat. The five paths are how it reaches shore.
PRAXIS Manifesto Section 05 — Power
Technical Infrastructure

Harvest the ecosystem. Do not reinvent it.

PRAXIS is not a single application. It is a strategy for composing existing open-source tools into a coherent infrastructure for democratic self-governance. The ecosystem of resilient, democratic, and decentralized technology is already large and growing. Dozens of mature, battle-tested projects already do what PRAXIS needs. The work is curation, integration, and training, not development from scratch. Every hour spent rebuilding something that already exists is an hour not spent organizing.

The principle: adopt before you adapt, adapt before you build. If an open-source tool does 80% of what an assembly needs, use it. If it needs modification, contribute upstream. Only build from scratch when nothing in the ecosystem addresses the need. The technology layer of PRAXIS is a recommended stack, not a proprietary platform. Different cities will use different tools based on local capacity, existing infrastructure, and community preference. What is shared is the methodology, the governance process, and the federation protocol. Not the software.
Governance and Deliberation
Tools for making real decisions together
Decidim is a modular participatory democracy framework used by cities and movements worldwide for consultations, participatory budgeting, proposals, and assemblies. It is the most mature open-source governance platform available. Consul Democracy offers a similar feature set and is deployed by over 250 cities globally, originally built by the Madrid city council. Loomio, born from the Occupy movement and run as a worker cooperative, handles collaborative decision-making for smaller groups through proposals, polls, and structured discussion. Pol.is uses computational methods to surface consensus and division across large groups and was used by Taiwan's digital democracy program to craft national policy through open deliberation. For communities that want liquid democracy (where you can delegate your vote to someone you trust on specific topics and revoke that delegation at any time), open-source platforms exist for that too. For cryptographically verifiable voting that preserves ballot secrecy, projects using zero-knowledge proofs now support over 300 organizations. No assembly needs to build its own governance software. The tools exist. The question is which combination fits the community.
Communication and Coordination
Talk to each other. Securely. Without corporate intermediaries.
Matrix is an open standard for decentralized, end-to-end encrypted communication. It federates (your server talks to other servers), it self-hosts (you control your data), and it is used by the French and German governments for internal communication. Element is the primary client. Signal is the gold standard for encrypted messaging and calls, used by hundreds of millions of people, operated by a nonprofit, and audited repeatedly. For communities that want no user identifiers at all (not even random IDs), SimpleX Chat provides messaging where messages are held temporarily on relays and then permanently deleted, with no accounts, no phone numbers, no metadata trail. Briar can sync messages over Tor, Wi-Fi, or Bluetooth, functioning when the internet is completely down. For organizing events without Facebook, Mobilizon is a federated event platform (nearly 400,000 events created across 79 instances) that handles group coordination, member management, and resource sharing.
Resilient Networking
When the internet goes down, the network stays up
Meshtastic is a decentralized, long-range mesh networking protocol using inexpensive LoRa radio hardware. Messages hop between nodes across miles without towers, ISPs, or internet infrastructure. It is the most popular open-source LoRa mesh project and is actively used for off-grid communication and disaster response. Reticulum is a networking stack that runs over LoRa, packet radio, Wi-Fi, Ethernet, serial, or optical links, operating at bandwidths from 150 bits per second to 40 megabits. It requires no hierarchical infrastructure, and its MeshChat application provides decentralized messaging across any of those transports. Yggdrasil is an encrypted overlay network that forms a self-healing mesh, transport-agnostic and requiring no central servers. For a community that wants to be prepared for infrastructure collapse, these tools are inexpensive, proven, and deployable by small groups with basic technical knowledge. They are not daily-use tools for most people. They are the backstop for when everything else fails.
Cooperative Economy and Mutual Aid
The economic infrastructure already exists
Open Food Network is an open-source marketplace connecting local farmers, food hubs, and cooperatives, used by 2,500 enterprises across 20 countries. Time banking platforms support hundreds of community time banks worldwide, where members earn hour-credits for sharing services. Community exchange networks operate across 1,300 exchanges in over 100 countries, enabling mutual credit and local currency systems. Hylo is a coordination platform for purpose-driven groups with discussions, requests, offers, projects, events, and geographic mapping. Open Collective provides transparent fundraising and budget management where every transaction is public, used by thousands of open-source projects and community organizations. For community relationship management (tracking members, organizing outreach, coordinating volunteers), CiviCRM is a mature, open-source system used by nonprofits and advocacy organizations worldwide. These tools do not need to be built. They need to be adopted, connected, and made accessible to communities that do not yet know they exist.
Movement-Owned Media
Algorithm-free, community-controlled, federated
PeerTube is decentralized, federated video hosting with no ads and no tracking, developed by a French nonprofit. Pixelfed is a decentralized Instagram alternative with chronological feeds and no algorithmic manipulation. Funkwhale is decentralized music sharing. WriteFreely is minimalist federated blogging. Ghost is an open-source publishing platform with built-in newsletters and 100,000 active websites. Lemmy is a federated discussion platform. All of these speak ActivityPub, meaning they interconnect with each other and with Mastodon. A video posted on PeerTube is visible from Mastodon. A blog post on WriteFreely appears in Mastodon timelines. The infrastructure for movement-owned media that is free from corporate algorithms, advertiser incentives, and platform risk already exists across hundreds of federated instances. PRAXIS does not need to build a media platform. The media platform is the Fediverse, and it is already running.
Mapping, Data, and Visibility
Making the work visible to itself
OpenStreetMap is the world's largest open geographic database, maintained by millions of contributors. Ushahidi is a crisis mapping platform that collects user-generated reports and maps them geographically, used since 2008 for disaster response, election monitoring, and community reporting. uMap lets anyone create interactive custom maps with no coding required (over 1.3 million maps created). For analytics dashboards that make governance data visible to non-technical users, Metabase provides visual query building and sharing, and Apache Superset handles enterprise-grade data exploration. An assembly that publishes its budget allocations, participation metrics, and outcome data on a public dashboard is an assembly that builds trust through transparency. These tools make that possible without custom development.
Education and Knowledge
Training facilitators, onboarding participants, sharing what works
Moodle is the most widely deployed open-source learning management system in the world, used by universities, organizations, and movements for structured education. BookStack is a self-hosted knowledge base organized by books, chapters, and pages, ideal for assembly playbooks, facilitation guides, and onboarding documentation. Wiki.js is a modern wiki platform with collaborative editing and Git-backed storage. The Zapatistas built autonomous education systems with chalkboards and community-selected teachers. PRAXIS can build them with open-source learning platforms that any community can deploy and customize.
The Integration Layer
What PRAXIS actually builds
If the ecosystem provides the tools, what does PRAXIS itself build? Three things. First, the Civic Engine: the engagement and progression layer that wraps civic participation in quests, roles, and Civic Points. No existing tool does this. Second, the assembly playbook: the methodology, facilitation guides, and governance processes that tell a new city how to run their first assembly, how to structure proposals, how to facilitate deliberation, and how to translate decisions into action. This is documentation and training, not software. Third, the integration glue: the lightweight connectors, deployment guides, and recommended configurations that compose these independent tools into a coherent experience for a specific community. A city might run Decidim for budgeting, Matrix for coordination, PeerTube for media, Meshtastic for emergency backup, and BookStack for their facilitation handbook. PRAXIS is the name for the practice of using all of them together in service of democratic self-governance.
Progressive resilience through composition
The resilience of this approach is architectural. If one tool fails or is compromised, the community switches to an alternative in the same category. If Decidim stops being maintained, Consul Democracy or Loomio takes its place. If a communication platform is surveilled, the community migrates to another. No single tool is a dependency. No single developer or organization is a bottleneck. The ecosystem is the resilience. This is the same principle that makes biological ecosystems robust: diversity of species, redundancy of function, no single point of failure. A monoculture is fragile. An ecosystem adapts.
Overthrow: Simulation Layer
Cooperative simulation as recruitment and training infrastructure
Overthrow is a cooperative simulation game built in Godot that models organizing dynamics: crowd behavior, resource allocation, coalition formation. It serves as a recruitment tool through which new participants develop intuition for democratic governance before attending their first assembly, a narrative and lore layer for the Civic Engine's world-building, and content for streaming audiences. Overthrow is a separate project that feeds into PRAXIS, not a dependency of it.
PRAXIS Manifesto Section 06 — Technology
Engagement & Progression

The Civic Engine.

The engagement layer that converts real-world civic participation into a structured, visible progression system, and converts participants into organizers. The Civic Engine is not a simulation of democratic practice. It is actual democratic practice, with a progression layer on top that makes the work legible, compelling, and communal for people who have never attended an assembly, canvassed a block, or thought about how their city actually governs itself.

Identity precedes action. People join movements because they want to be someone, not merely to do something. The Civic Engine provides a role, a progression path, and a place in something larger than immediate circumstances, before the participant has any knowledge of the underlying political framework. By the time a new participant completes the first quest chain, they have done more civic engagement than most Americans complete in a decade, and they have a local network they did not have before. The Zapatistas understood this: "We cover our faces to be seen." Identity within a collective is the precondition for sustained action.
The Twelve Classes
This is a real-world ARG. There are no dice, no ability scores, no hit points. The "game actions" are actual organizing actions. Progression is earned through real experience in the field. Every class corresponds to a function that democratic movements need. Every class has a core mechanic that drives its play pattern.
Community Presence
First Responder
The person who shows up. First to the march, first to the mutual aid distribution, first to stand between a neighbor and a bad situation. Core mechanic: Rage, the refusal to look away when something is wrong. Channels that refusal into presence, solidarity, and action. In Rojava, community defense volunteers work regular jobs by day and look after their neighborhoods at night.
Narrative & Storytelling
Culture Jammer
Reframes the conversation. Memes, remixes, public spectacle, the ability to make people see what they have been trained to ignore. Core mechanic: Inspiration, creative acts that shift how people understand what is happening around them. Makes the movement the most interesting thing happening in a given city in a given week.
Care & Wellbeing
Medic
Sustains people through difficult work. Emotional support, harm reduction, burnout prevention, making sure everyone gets home safe. Core mechanic: Triage, the ability to assess who needs what and provide it under pressure. The person who checks on you the morning after. Every movement runs on care, and care is skilled labor.
Land & Ecology
Eco-Warrior Gardener
Builds the physical commons. Community gardens, regenerative agriculture, land stewardship, ecological restoration. Core mechanic: Rootedness, drawn to the long game. The Zapatistas' organic farming and the community land trust movement are both expressions of this class. Thinks in growing seasons, not news cycles.
Coordination & Logistics
Field Coordinator
Turns good intentions into effective operations. Event logistics, action coordination, contingency planning, making sure the right people are in the right place at the right time. Core mechanic: Readiness, always prepared, never improvising when preparation was possible. The difference between a crowd and a coordinated effort.
Relationship-Building
Organizer Ascetic
Does the relational work that transforms a contact list into a community. Knocks on doors, builds trust over years, shows up consistently. Core mechanic: Sacrifice, sustained commitment over personal convenience. The Zapatistas spent a decade doing this in 500 communities before anyone outside Chiapas heard their name. The class that makes all other classes possible at scale.
Moral Clarity
Vanguard
Holds the line on principles when the pressure to compromise is strongest. Not a leader who commands but a compass that does not move. Core mechanic: Oath, bound to the values that brought everyone into the work. When the path forward is unclear, the Vanguard reminds the assembly why it exists.
Research & Investigation
Scout
Gathers the information that makes effective action possible. Public records research, power structure mapping, understanding who makes decisions and who benefits. Core mechanic: Deep Knowledge, knowing how specific systems and institutions actually work. The Scout knows which council member owns rental property in the district and which journalist will run the story.
Accountability & Transparency
Saboteur
Holds powerful institutions accountable by making hidden information public. Freedom of Information requests, public records campaigns, documenting what officials say versus what they do. Core mechanic: Exposure, finding the structural vulnerabilities in unjust systems and making them visible. The whistleblower, the leak, the FOIA that unravels the cover-up.
Cultural Production
Artist
Builds the culture of the world being born. Murals, songs, theater, design, the work that outlives any single campaign. Core mechanic: Craft, art that outlives the artist. Where the Culture Jammer disrupts the existing narrative, the Artist builds the new one. Beauty as evidence that another world is possible.
Creative Advocacy
Absurdist
Exposes hypocrisy by taking institutional logic to its natural conclusion. Files the public comment that grinds the permit process to a halt. Attends the town hall in a costume that makes the absurdity of the policy impossible to ignore. Core mechanic: Pact, understands institutional rules well enough to use them creatively. Humor as a political tool.
Tools & Systems
Techie
Keeps the infrastructure running. Sets up the communication channels, builds the dashboards, writes the guides, runs the numbers. Core mechanic: Spellbook, technical knowledge and documentation as a form of power. The class whose work is invisible until it breaks. Every other class depends on this one and rarely says thank you.
Cross-Class Synergies
Classes are not silos. The most effective civic actions emerge from deliberate combinations. First Responder + Field Coordinator: community presence backed by disciplined coordination. Medic + Organizer Ascetic: sustainable long-haul organizing with care infrastructure that prevents burnout. Scout + Saboteur: research that leads to accountability. Culture Jammer + Artist: narrative disruption backed by cultural production that outlasts the news cycle. Eco-Warrior Gardener + Vanguard: land stewardship grounded in unshakable principle. Absurdist + Techie: institutional systems documented and creatively exposed. These are not hypothetical pairings. They are the combinations that have produced results in every movement that understood that a diversity of approaches requires a diversity of roles.
Team Composition
A balanced team covers six functions
A full organizing team should have at minimum: one presence (First Responder, Field Coordinator, or Vanguard), one support (Medic or Organizer Ascetic), one research (Scout or Techie), one accountability (Saboteur or Absurdist), one narrative (Culture Jammer or Artist), and one wildcard (Eco-Warrior Gardener or any class the local context demands). Not every team will have six people. Not every team will be balanced. But a network that is missing an entire function will eventually feel the gap. The class system makes the gap visible before it becomes a crisis.

Quest Engine: Four Quest Types
I
Onboarding Quests: First 30 Days
Attend a first assembly. Connect with three other participants in the same city. Map the power structure in one neighborhood. Complete a first civic action. Choose a class. By completion, the new participant has built a local network, contributed to a civic record, and established a progression identity within the Civic Engine, all before understanding the theoretical framework underlying the work.
II
Class Quests: Ongoing Progression
Each class maintains its own progression track with unlockable capabilities at higher levels. Medics advance through trauma support competencies. Scouts unlock network-wide intelligence tools. Techies unlock deployment and infrastructure resources. Saboteurs unlock secure communication protocols. First Responders unlock legal support coordination. Culture Jammers unlock production resources and distribution channels. Twelve distinct progression paths create long-term engagement depth for committed participants without raising the entry barrier for newcomers.
III
Campaign Quests: Time-Limited, Real-World Stakes
Tied directly to actual political events. A city council housing vote becomes an active campaign quest. An election cycle becomes the Civic Engine's largest quest chain of the year. A natural disaster triggers emergency mutual aid quest activation. Real deadlines, real decisions, real outcomes determine quest success. This is the precise distinction between PRAXIS and every civic engagement app that preceded it.
IV
Network Quests: Cross-City Coordination
Certain quest chains require simultaneous completion by participants across multiple federated cities. A coordinated national day of action. A synchronized mutual aid response. A cross-city legislative campaign. Network quests produce the lived experience of operating within something larger than one city, and generate the collective political force that only emerges from coordinated action across geography.
Physical / Digital Integration
The Civic Engine operates in physical space as fully as it operates digitally. QR codes at civic locations activate location-based quests. Displays at cooperative businesses show current city quest status and network progress. In-person events (citizen assemblies, mutual aid distributions, cooperative launches) constitute the highest-value quest completions in the system. The design objective is the moment when participants who found each other online encounter each other in physical space for the first time. That moment is the movement becoming real to itself.

PRAXIS Manifesto Section 08 — Communications
Scaling Architecture

Federation architecture for national scale.

Every successful dual power movement has encountered the same structural bottleneck: how to expand from one city to many without either centralizing control (which destroys the democratic character) or fragmenting into disconnected local projects that never achieve critical mass. The Zapatistas solved this by creating regional governance centers where autonomous municipalities coordinate without any center having authority over the others. Rojava solved it through ascending tiers of communes, councils, and cantons, each level serving the one below it. PRAXIS implements the same principle as a technical protocol.

Five Scaling Channels: Operating Simultaneously
1
The Practice as Scaling Mechanism
Once proven in the founding city, PRAXIS becomes a replicable practice that any group in any city can adopt independently and connect to the network. The Assembly Playbook, tool guides, and Civic Engine are freely available. Proposals that succeed in one city become templates available to the network. The movement becomes legible to itself across geography without requiring PRAXIS leadership to be physically present in each new city.
2
Academic Pipeline
Research partnerships publish assembly outcomes → findings enter academic discourse → other researchers seek to replicate the study → they provide local institutional access, research funding, and academic legitimacy → PRAXIS provides methodology and tool recommendations. Primary target: the Participatory Budgeting Project, which supports active PB processes across the US, all potential PRAXIS deployments.
3
Existing Movement Networks
Trusted organizers in multiple cities who carry trust based on demonstrated shared history. The ask is not organizational membership. The ask is: deploy PRAXIS in your city and federate with the network. Ten active nodes become fifty through organic growth at each node.
4
The Civic Engine as Viral Infrastructure
The engagement system scales through word of mouth and digital discovery without requiring direct outreach at each point of expansion. A participant in any city encounters PRAXIS online. The onboarding quest chain requires real-world actions in their own city: attend a local government meeting, map the power structure of their neighborhood, connect with three other local participants. The engagement layer is the organizing tool. Quest chain completion produces a local network of engaged people who become the nucleus of a local assembly, without platform leadership present.
5
The Crisis as Accelerant
Under crisis conditions, the organizing challenge is not generating urgency. It is being prepared to absorb the people that urgency is already producing. Minneapolis 2020 demonstrated what happens when crisis energy meets no institutional infrastructure: temporary mutual aid, then exhaustion, then reversion. PRAXIS needs to be deployable rapidly, onboarding must be frictionless, and the first encounter must be compelling enough to retain participants beyond the initial crisis moment.
Coalition Targets: Ordered by Strategic Alignment
Cooperative economy networks
Worker cooperative federations, democracy-at-work institutes, local cooperative development organizations. Already building the economics domain, the same layer Rojava implements through its cooperatives and the Zapatistas through their solidarity trade networks. They require governance and technology infrastructure. PRAXIS provides that.
Mutual aid networks
Networks established during the COVID-19 pandemic remain active but fragmented. Minneapolis showed that volunteer-run mutual aid can serve 18,000 people in weeks, and collapse within months without institutional support. They require coordination tools, resource tracking infrastructure, and cross-city federation. They constitute the lowest-friction first deployment target in most cities nationally.
Tenant unions and housing justice organizations
The housing crisis is the most broadly radicalizing economic force operating in US cities. Housing justice organizations in every major city are natural alignment partners.
Indigenous sovereignty movements
Tribal nations practicing active self-governance constitute the longest-running dual power experiment in North America. The federation model respects sovereign autonomy while providing alliance infrastructure.
Reform labor unions
Recent organizing waves produced a generation of workers with collective action experience but no infrastructure beyond their specific workplace. PRAXIS provides the broader democratic framework into which that experience can be integrated and scaled.
Immigrant rights organizations
Under federal enforcement conditions, immigrant communities are constructing resilience infrastructure out of operational necessity. The mesh networking layer (communication that functions when conventional channels are surveilled or disrupted) is directly and immediately relevant.
Climate justice organizations
Community disaster resilience infrastructure serves climate adaptation and political crisis response simultaneously. Organizations building preparedness for climate events are building the same infrastructure required for political crisis. The convergence is architectural, not rhetorical, the same convergence Bookchin identified in social ecology.
Antiwar movement
War generates organizing energy that currently lacks constructive institutional outlets. PRAXIS provides a framework that channels antiwar energy into the democratic infrastructure capable of preventing future wars, not only opposing the current one.
International Federation
European cities are already building democratic governance platforms on compatible infrastructure. Barcelona runs its own open-source participatory democracy platform. Helsinki runs a city-scale participatory budgeting process. Dozens of other European municipalities are experimenting with digital direct democracy. International federation creates resilience against national-level repression: disruption of domestic nodes does not terminate international nodes. Rojava's vulnerability to Turkish invasion and economic embargo demonstrates what happens when democratic confederalism operates within a single national context without international structural support. The model survives because it is not contained within any single national jurisdiction, the same distributed resilience principle as the mesh network, applied at geopolitical scale.

PRAXIS Manifesto Section 10 — Implementation
Internal Structure

Working groups: how the build is organized.

PRAXIS is built by working groups, not committees. Each group owns a domain, makes decisions within that domain, and reports to the network through regular coordination. Groups operate with maximum autonomy and minimum bureaucracy. No group requires permission from another to act within its scope. Coordination happens through shared visibility, not hierarchical approval.

The Five Working Groups
01
Platform: Engineering & Product
Owns: The three things PRAXIS builds: the Civic Engine, the integration glue between ecosystem tools, and the technical side of deployment guides. Also maintains recommended tool configurations, evaluates new tools entering the ecosystem, and provides tech support to communities setting up their infrastructure. Ships: The Civic Engine software, integration connectors, deployment guides, and tool recommendations. Coordination: Weekly standup (async or sync). Decisions about which tools to recommend, how to connect them, and Civic Engine development are made here. Non-technical groups surface requirements; this group determines how to address them. Staffing: Requires at minimum one developer. Scales with contributors as the Civic Engine codebase grows.
02
Governance: Assembly Design & Democratic Practice
Owns: How assemblies actually work: facilitation methodology, decision-making process, proposal formats, voting procedures, and the translation of democratic theory into repeatable practice. Writes the assembly playbook that any new city can follow. Ships: Facilitation guides, assembly templates, training materials, and documented outcomes from live assemblies. Coordination: Biweekly. Feeds requirements to Platform (what the software needs to support) and to Communications (what outcomes to document). Staffing: Experienced facilitators, organizers with assembly experience, and participants willing to test and iterate on process.
03
Communications: Media & Narrative
Owns: Everything the public sees: visual identity, podcast, newsletter, short-form video, press strategy, and PRAXIS's social presence. Also owns Culture Jammer and Artist production infrastructure and tooling. Ships: Content on a consistent schedule. The manifesto, the podcast, the Dispatch newsletter, assembly documentation video, and all press materials. Coordination: Weekly editorial planning. Works closely with Governance (documenting assemblies) and Coalition (amplifying partnership stories). Staffing: Requires at minimum one person who can produce video. Scales with Creators recruited through the civic engine.
04
Coalition: Outreach & Federation
Owns: Relationships with external organizations, partnership development, new city node recruitment, and the federation pipeline. Maps the coalition landscape, initiates contact, and manages ongoing relationships with cooperative networks, mutual aid organizations, tenant unions, academic partners, and PBP. Ships: Signed partnerships, new city node organizing committees, and a growing network map. Coordination: Biweekly. Reports new city interest to Platform (deployment support needed) and to Governance (assembly methodology handoff). Staffing: Organizers with existing movement relationships. The ten-city outreach in Phase 0 is this group's founding deliverable.
05
Research: Data & Academic Partnerships
Owns: Outcome measurement, participation analytics, academic publication pipeline, and grant impact reporting. Turns assembly outcomes into publishable research that generates academic legitimacy and opens institutional doors. Also manages the grant writing and submission pipeline. Ships: Quarterly data reports, grant applications, and academic papers. Coordination: Monthly. Feeds data to Communications (for the Research Brief newsletter tier) and to Coalition (for partnership credibility). Staffing: Requires an academic research lead with institutional access. Scales with research assistants and partner university involvement.
Coordination Structure
Autonomy within scope, visibility across groups
Each working group makes decisions independently within its domain. Cross-group coordination happens through two mechanisms: a shared project board visible to all groups (what each group is working on, what's blocked, what's shipped) and a monthly all-groups sync where each group presents a five-minute status update and raises cross-group dependencies. The monthly sync is the only required meeting. Everything else is internal to each group. If a decision affects another group's domain, the groups coordinate directly, no approval chain, no steering committee. The technical lead has final say on tool recommendations. The governance group has final say on democratic process. The communications group has final say on public-facing narrative. Disputes between groups are resolved through the same democratic deliberation PRAXIS is designed to facilitate.
Scaling working groups as the network grows
In Phase 0, the working groups may each have one to three people; some groups may be a single person wearing multiple hats. That is fine. The structure exists so that as the network grows, new contributors have a clear place to plug in: a developer joins Platform, a filmmaker joins Communications, an academic joins Research, a connected organizer joins Coalition. The working group structure is the onboarding path for contributors, not a bureaucratic layer for a small team. When the structure no longer serves the work, restructure it. The Zapatistas have done this three times in thirty years and are stronger for it.
